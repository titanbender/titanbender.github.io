Accidents during the day

Traffic flow changes during the day. Therefore, it seems obvious to explore if traffic accidents indeed happen more frequently during morning and afternoon traffic. Does this pattern exist for both non-lethal and lethal accidents? Try to explore by hitting the buttons below. If you spend enough time, you might find that certain districts follow specific patterns while other districts behave more randomly. Non-fatal traffic accidents generally occour frequent during the morning up to late afternoon, whereas it decreases drastically. Fatal traffic accidents, on the other hand, are much more evenly distributed throughout the hours of the day. This could be intreprated as even though there is less traffic during the late hours of the day, fatal accidents occur just as frequent because of other factors such as limited visibality, intoxicated drivers, pedestrians or bicyclists that are more prevelant during those hours.








Seasonality

So we loooked at the specific hours of the day. What about the seasonality over the year? For example does accidents happen more frequently in February than July? Are there certain months that are more dangerous for bicyclists? This time we created a time series to give you the opportunity to explore traffic accidents involving Pedestrians, Cyclists, and Motorists vary 5 New York City Boroughs throughout the year. Here we see a clear pattern for teh aggregated accident data: Accidents happen more frequently during the summer months compared to the winter. However, for pedistrians the risk is almost the same throughout the season. The pattern emerges from the fact that cyclists and motorists follow this trend. What is the reason for this? It's tough to predict. Personally, I would have expected the winter months to be more dangerous as NYC is known for snow and ice during this season.

Weather

So we brought up weather in the seasonility exploration as we expected more accidents during the winter months, but you can't really argue with data. One explanation that counter the impact of bad weather is the fact that drivers might be more careful during those conditions. To get a sense of whether a rainy day impacts the number of accidents, we've done some data analysis. Below you see a graph displaying number of accidents and the amount of rain on particular days. The graph doesn't show a clear pattern, which is also supported by the fact that the pearson correlation, an indicator of a linear relationsship, is close to 0, which indicates no linear relation.





K nearest neighbors (KNN)

So, if NYC gets a report about an accident is it possible to predict the severity of the accident based on just the geographic location? Using KNN, we're able to create a grid over the city with predictions for each locations. If you're a New Yorker and have a specific route to work every day, this could show some disturbing results. Therefore, the results are saved in a different page, so that we do not disturb your sleep tonight unless you're willing to take the chance. "Pssst - Fear Not". Since non-fatal accidents are overrepresented in the dataset, almost all prediction in the unbalanced dataset are of non-fatal character. However, we've also created a unbalanced calculations with an equal number of non-lethal and lethal data sampled. Don't be too worried when you view this map.
